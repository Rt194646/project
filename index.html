Change Over Time: Video Games and Public Perception (1995–2010)

Introduction Claim Timeline Major Changes Public Perception Sources

Introduction

Between 1995 and 2010, video game graphics evolved dramatically—from pixelated art to cinematic realism. This project explores how visual advancements changed the way society views video games.

Claim

Between 1995 and 2010, improvements in graphics technology fundamentally shifted public perception of games—from childlike entertainment to serious, artistic content.

Key Evidence Timeline

1995: Doom

Doom used pixel-art and early 3D rendering. Game visuals felt groundbreaking but still primitive.

1995: Doom (PlayStation)

Later ports, like the PlayStation version, added simple lighting effects and colors.

2000: Halo

Halo: Combat Evolved offered immersive 3D, smooth environments, and richer storytelling.

2010: Red Dead Redemption

Red Dead Redemption achieved cinematic realism using HD textures, motion capture, and dynamic lighting.

Major Changes

  • Technology: Rise of GPU, advanced lighting, motion capture
  • Design: Shift from pixel/art style to immersive storytelling
  • Audience: Broader age groups, including adults
  • Debates: Emerging concerns about realism, violence, and screen time

Public Perception Timeline

  • 1995: “Games are just fun.”
  • 2000: “They’re cool, mainstream.”
  • 2010: “They’re art, but also contentious.”

Key insight: Technology drives cultural perception—realism leads to respect, but also controversy.
